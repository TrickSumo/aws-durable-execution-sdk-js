# Operator at Scale — 5Q

Operator at Scale delivers workflow observability for AWS Lambda Durable Execution, enabling customers to find, inspect, and understand their workflow executions at production scale. Rather than building a proprietary internal store with a custom query API, we emit curated execution data to customer-owned destinations (S3, DynamoDB, CloudWatch Logs, Aurora, or third-party platforms) and provide a Workflow Insight UI that queries their data directly. The system is fully configurable — opt-in, sampling-aware, destination-agnostic, and extensible via plugins — so customers pay only for the observability they need.

---

## 1. Who is the customer and what insights do we have about them?

We serve two primary customer categories:

### Operators (Operational Health & Reliability)

Engineers, SREs, and on-call teams responsible for keeping workflows running reliably in production. They need to detect problems, diagnose failures, and act on them — often under time pressure during incidents.

**What they ask:**

- "Are any workflows stuck or failing right now?"
- "Which step failed and why?"
- "Is a downstream service causing failures?"
- "Cancel all 10,000 stuck workflows from last night's outage"
- "How long does it take to resume after a callback arrives?"

### Business Analysts (Workflow Intelligence & Reporting)

Product managers, business analysts, data teams, and engineering managers who use workflow execution data to understand business outcomes, generate reports, and make decisions.

**What they ask:**

- "What percentage of insurance claims are rejected each day?"
- "What's the average processing time by region and customer tier?"
- "Which workflow variant has the highest cost?"
- "Show me a weekly trend of automation rate (straight-through vs manual intervention)"
- "How much does each workflow type cost us per month?"

## 2. What is the prevailing customer problem/opportunity? What data informed this?

Today, customers who need operational or business intelligence data from their durable executions can only rely on the execution history events API. This API has low rate limits, returns raw event-level data for a single execution at a time, and provides no aggregation, filtering, or cross-execution query capabilities.

**The problems customers face:**

- **Raw data at massive scale is unusable for cross-execution queries** — Each execution can produce hundreds or thousands of history events. At production scale, this grows to billions of items. Questions that require correlating data across executions — "which workflows failed after retrying the payment step 3 times?", "what's the average duration of workflows that went through the manual review path?", "show me all executions for customer X that timed out waiting for a callback" — require fetching events per-execution, linking them together, reconstructing workflow state, and then aggregating across thousands of reconstructed executions. This is slow, expensive, and requires building custom ETL pipelines. A simple metric can tell you _how many_ failed, but not _why_, _which step_, or _under what conditions_.

- **No support for storing execution data in other AWS services** — Customers cannot direct execution data to S3, DynamoDB, Aurora, CloudWatch Logs, or any other AWS storage service. There is no built-in path from execution events to the customer's preferred data store. Customers who want their data in S3 for Athena queries or DynamoDB for fast lookups must build and maintain their own extraction and loading infrastructure.

- **No support for third-party destinations** — Customers using Datadog, Splunk, Snowflake, Grafana Cloud, or other observability platforms have no way to send execution data directly to those systems. They must first extract raw events via the API (with its rate limits), transform them, and push them to their platform — a fragile, costly pipeline they must build and operate themselves.

- **No curated, query-ready data** — The raw history events are optimized for replay and recovery, not for human queries. There is no pre-processed, denormalized view that answers common questions without complex joins across thousands of event records.

**Data that informed this:**

- Research across 7 competing workflow platforms (Temporal, Restate, DBOS, Cloudflare, Azure Durable, Vercel), their roadmaps, blog posts, and community-reported issues
- Research across 25+ service types (mainframe batch, trading systems, IoT, healthcare, ETL, CI/CD) identifying 34 distinct observability requirements
- Direct customer meetings — both Durable Functions customers and AWS Step Functions customers who face similar limitations
- Customer requests submitted through our interest channel

## 3. What is the solution? Why is it the right solution versus alternatives?

### What we will deliver

**A configurable, opt-in observability layer** that emits curated execution data to customer-chosen destinations without affecting workflow execution performance. The solution has five key components:

**1. Curated summary data per execution**

We generate a pre-processed, denormalized summary record for each execution — not raw history events. Raw events are useful for inspecting a single execution in detail, but they are the wrong data model for querying across multiple executions. Each execution can produce hundreds or thousands of events, and at scale this grows to billions of items. Answering cross-execution questions ("how many failed today?", "what's the average duration by region?") from raw events requires fetching per-execution, joining across event types, and reconstructing state — slow, expensive, and impractical at production scale. The curated summary is a single flat record per execution containing status, duration, step outcomes, error details, and custom fields — ready for filtering and aggregation without joins.

**2. AWS service destinations**

Customers can store execution data in their preferred AWS services, choosing one or multiple simultaneously:

- **S3** — cost-effective archival, queryable via Athena
- **DynamoDB** — fast lookups by execution ID, TTL-based retention
- **Aurora / RDS** — full SQL querying and complex filtering
- **CloudWatch Logs** — zero-setup integration with existing monitoring and Log Insights
- **Kinesis Data Streams / Firehose** — real-time streaming to multiple consumers
- **EventBridge** — event-driven routing to trigger reactions on workflow lifecycle events

**3. Third-party destination support**

Customers can send data directly to external platforms without routing through an intermediate AWS service:

- Datadog
- Splunk
- Snowflake
- Grafana Cloud
- New Relic
- Custom HTTPS webhook endpoints

**4. OpenTelemetry (OTel) log support**

We support emitting execution data as OTel-formatted log records, enabling customers to send data to any third-party platform that accepts OpenTelemetry out of the box — without needing a platform-specific plugin. The curated JSON record is emitted as the log record body (OTel's body field supports structured map/JSON data natively), with key fields (execution ID, function name, status) also set as OTel attributes for indexing and filtering at the backend. This provides universal compatibility with the growing ecosystem of OTel-native tools — Datadog, Grafana Loki, Splunk, New Relic, and any OTLP-compatible collector.

**5. Configurable and non-intrusive**

The entire system is opt-in and configurable: sampling rates, data fields included/excluded, in-progress vs finished-only, destination selection, and size limits. The emission pipeline is asynchronous and non-blocking — a slow or unavailable destination never impacts workflow execution latency or reliability.

**6. Pipeline health observability ("where is my data?")**

Customers can enable CloudWatch Metrics to monitor the emission pipeline itself. Metrics include: records emitted, records dropped, destination errors, and emission latency — broken down by destination. This answers the inevitable "where is my data?" question: if records are being dropped or the destination is returning errors, the customer sees it immediately via CloudWatch Alarms rather than discovering it hours later when the Insight UI shows no data. Metric names, namespaces, and dimensions are customizable to fit existing monitoring conventions.

**7. Workflow Insight UI**

We provide a purpose-built UI for querying and visualizing execution data. Unlike general-purpose tools like Grafana or Kibana, our UI is schema-aware — it understands the structure of curated execution records (status, duration, steps, errors, custom fields) and provides workflow-specific interactions: filter by execution status, drill into step timelines, compare durations across workflow types, and search using natural language ("show me all failed payment workflows this week"). Customers don't need to write queries or configure dashboards from scratch — the UI works out of the box because it knows what the data looks like.

This does not replace general-purpose tools. Customers who prefer Grafana, Datadog, or Athena can query their data directly with those tools — the data is in their storage either way. The Workflow Insight UI is one additional option in the customer's arsenal: the fastest path to answers for workflow-specific questions, without requiring query language expertise or dashboard setup.

We will also provide the UI as an open-source component library that customers can modify, extend, and embed into their own applications. Teams that need custom dashboards — branded operations portals, internal tools with workflow views alongside other business data, or specialized views for specific workflow types — can use our components as building blocks rather than starting from scratch. They get pre-built widgets (execution table, step timeline, duration charts, status filters) that understand the curated data schema, and full freedom to compose them into whatever layout their use case requires.

### Why this is the right solution versus alternatives

**Why not just expose raw execution events?** Raw history events work well for investigating a single execution — you can see every step, every retry, every state transition in detail. But they are fundamentally the wrong data model for cross-execution queries. A single execution can produce hundreds or thousands of events. At production scale with millions of executions, this becomes billions of event records. To answer "how many workflows failed today?" a customer would need to scan every execution's events, find the terminal event, extract the status, and aggregate. To answer "what's the average duration by region?" they'd need to join events across executions, correlate start/end timestamps, and group by custom attributes. This is prohibitively slow and expensive — it's like reading every page of every book in a library to count how many books are about history. Curated summary records give customers a single flat row per execution that's immediately filterable and aggregatable.

**Why not store data internally in our service?** If we store execution data in our own internal database, we become the gatekeeper of customer data. Customers can't query it with their own tools, can't join it with their other business data, can't control retention or encryption, and can't delete it without asking us. It creates vendor lock-in, adds operational burden on our side (we'd need to scale, secure, and maintain a query-serving database), and forces us to build access controls, export mechanisms, and compliance features that customers already have in their own infrastructure. By emitting to customer-owned storage, the customer has full control from day one.

**Why not build our own query API and query language?** Building a query API means inventing a query language (or adopting SQL/GraphQL), building query infrastructure that scales with customer data volume, handling authentication and authorization for query access, managing query performance and timeouts, and maintaining backward compatibility as the API evolves. Meanwhile, customers already have powerful query tools — Athena for S3, Log Insights for CloudWatch, SQL for Aurora, Datadog's query language for Datadog. Each of these is battle-tested, well-documented, and already understood by the customer's team. We would be building an inferior version of what already exists. Instead, we provide a Workflow Insight UI that translates user intent into the native query language of whatever destination the customer chose.

**Why not a single managed destination like Cloudflare?** Cloudflare Workflows stores data internally and exposes it through their REST API — simple, but inflexible. Customers can't choose their storage, can't control costs, can't integrate with existing tools, and can't run complex analytics. Different customers have fundamentally different needs: a startup wants zero-setup CloudWatch Logs; an enterprise wants S3 + Athena for compliance archival; a data team wants Snowflake for BI. Forcing one destination alienates everyone who doesn't use that specific tool.

**Why not always-on full-detail recording like Restate?** Restate records everything automatically with zero configuration — great for small deployments, but at scale it becomes expensive and creates resource contention (queries share I/O with the execution engine). High-volume customers processing millions of executions per day cannot afford to record full detail for every execution. Sampling, configurable field selection, and opt-in modes let customers choose their cost/detail trade-off rather than imposing one on everyone.

| Alternative                | Trade-off                                                     | Our approach                                   |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Raw events only            | Unusable at scale for cross-execution queries                 | Curated summary records, flat and aggregatable |
| Internal store + API       | Vendor lock-in, operational burden, inferior query tools      | Customer-owned storage, native query tools     |
| Custom query language      | Reinventing the wheel, maintenance burden, limited capability | Translate to destination-native queries        |
| Single managed destination | Inflexible, can't integrate with existing stacks              | Multi-destination, customer's choice           |
| Always-on full recording   | Expensive at scale, resource contention                       | Opt-in, sampling, configurable content         |

## 4. How would we describe the end-to-end customer experience? What is the most important customer benefit?

### End-to-end experience

**1. Configure:** A developer adds observability plug-in with configuration to their durable function. They specify: destination (e.g., S3 bucket, CloudWatch log group, or Datadog endpoint), data content (which fields to include), mode (in-progress or finished-only), and optionally sampling rate. For the simplest case, a single line enables defaults: emit to CloudWatch Logs, all fields, finished executions only.

**2. Execute:** The durable function runs as normal. Steps execute, waits suspend, callbacks resume. The SDK always maintains the up-to-date execution state (via [GetDurableExecutionState](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/lambda/latest/api/API_GetDurableExecutionState.html)) — this is the same state used for replay and recovery. The observability layer piggybacks on this existing state; it doesn't perform any separate data collection or heavy computation.

**3. Emit:** At the configured trigger point (each status change for in-progress mode, or upon completion for finished-only mode), the SDK plugin reads the already-available execution state, applies light transformations (field selection, truncation, custom field attachment), and delivers the curated record to the configured destination(s). There is no expensive aggregation or reconstruction — the execution state is already structured and current. For replay safety, only new progress triggers emission; replayed steps produce no duplicate records.

**4. Store:** The data lands in the customer's own storage. They own it, control retention, control access, and can delete it at will. If multiple destinations are configured, each receives its own copy (potentially with different field selections or sampling rates).

**5. Query:** The customer queries their data using the native tools of their chosen destination — Athena SQL for S3, Log Insights for CloudWatch, DynamoDB queries for DynamoDB, Datadog's query language for Datadog. No new query language to learn. No new API to call.

**6. Insight UI (optional):** For customers who want a faster path to answers, the Workflow Insight UI (in AWS Console, VS Code, or the open-source library) provides structured filters and natural language search. The UI queries the customer's storage directly using their IAM role — it's a convenience layer, not a separate data store. The customer types "show me all failed payment workflows this week" and the UI translates that into the appropriate query for their destination.

**7. Act:** Based on what they find, operators take action — cancel stuck workflows, retry failed ones, investigate downstream dependencies. Business analysts export data to dashboards, generate reports, or feed it into BI tools. The curated data format makes both paths straightforward.

### Most important customer benefit

Customers get production-scale workflow observability — find any execution, understand any failure, answer any business question — without building ETL pipelines, managing query infrastructure, or learning proprietary tools. Data flows to where they already work, in a format ready for the questions they actually ask.

### What the developer experience looks like

**Minimal setup (TypeScript):**

```typescript
import { withDurableExecution } from "@aws/durable-execution-sdk-js";
import { CloudWatchLogsInsight } from "@aws/durable-execution-sdk-js-insight";

export const handler = withDurableExecution(
  async (event, context) => {
    // workflow code unchanged
    const result = await context.step("process", async () =>
      processOrder(event),
    );
    await context.wait({ hours: 24 });
    await context.step("notify", async () => sendConfirmation(result));
    return result;
  },
  {
    plugins: [
      new CloudWatchLogsInsight({
        logGroupName: "/aws/durable/order-processing",
      }),
    ],
  },
);
```

**Advanced setup (multiple destinations, sampling, custom fields):**

```typescript
import { withDurableExecution } from "@aws/durable-execution-sdk-js";
import {
  S3Insight,
  CloudWatchLogsInsight,
} from "@aws/durable-execution-sdk-js-insight";

export const handler = withDurableExecution(
  async (event, context) => {
    // Attach custom fields for business-dimension queries
    context.insight.setAttributes({
      customerId: event.customerId,
      region: event.region,
      orderAmount: event.amount,
    });

    const result = await context.step("validate", async () => validate(event));
    await context.step("charge", async () => chargeCard(event));
    await context.step("fulfill", async () => fulfill(event));
    return result;
  },
  {
    plugins: [
      // Cheap archival — all executions, minimal fields
      new S3Insight({
        bucket: "my-workflow-data",
        prefix: "executions/",
        fields: ["status", "duration", "error", "customFields"],
      }),
      // Real-time debugging — 100% of executions, full detail
      new CloudWatchLogsInsight({
        logGroupName: "/aws/durable/orders",
        includeStepDetails: true,
        includeInputOutput: true,
        mode: "in-progress", // emit on every status change
      }),
    ],
  },
);
```

**Enterprise setup (configure once, apply everywhere):**

Enterprise customers with many durable functions don't embed plugin configuration in every handler. Instead, they define it once and share it across their fleet:

```typescript
// insight-config.ts — defined once, shared across all functions
import {
  S3Insight,
  CloudWatchLogsInsight,
  InsightFromEnvironment,
} from "@aws/durable-execution-sdk-js-insight";

export const insightPlugins = [
  new S3Insight({
    bucket: process.env.INSIGHT_BUCKET!,
    prefix: process.env.INSIGHT_PREFIX!,
  }),
  new CloudWatchLogsInsight({
    logGroupName: process.env.INSIGHT_LOG_GROUP!,
  }),
];
```

```typescript
// Any handler — just imports the shared config
import { withDurableExecution } from "@aws/durable-execution-sdk-js";
import { insightPlugins } from "../insight-config";

export const handler = withDurableExecution(
  async (event, context) => {
    // workflow code — no observability concerns here
    return await processOrder(event, context);
  },
  { plugins: insightPlugins },
);
```

```typescript
// Alternative: fully environment-driven (zero plugin code in handlers)
import { InsightFromEnvironment } from "@aws/durable-execution-sdk-js-insight";

export const handler = withDurableExecution(myWorkflow, {
  plugins: [InsightFromEnvironment()], // reads destination, fields, sampling from env vars
});
```

The plugin configuration is standard code — customers manage it the same way they manage any shared library config: a single module, environment variables, or infrastructure-as-code that sets Lambda environment variables across their fleet. No need to touch individual handler files when changing observability settings.

### What this leaves open

- **Server-side execution termination (STOPPED, TIMED_OUT)** — Some execution state changes happen server-side without triggering a new Lambda invocation (e.g., an execution is stopped via API or times out). In these cases, the plugin cannot run and will not update the record in the destination. The last emitted record will reflect the state at the time of the most recent Lambda invocation. Customers who need to capture these terminal states should subscribe to EventBridge events for durable execution lifecycle changes and update their destination accordingly.
- **Backfill of historical executions** — not in initial launch; customers see data only for executions started after enabling the feature.
- **Workflow Insight UI for third-party destinations** — the Console/VS Code UI works out-of-the-box for AWS destinations only. Third-party destination users implement a query adapter in the open-source UI library.
- **Bulk operations from the Insight UI** — v1 is read-only (find and inspect). Acting on workflows (cancel, retry) from the UI is a follow-on.

### What "done" looks like for v1

- OTel log emission works for third-party destinations

### Default experience (without Workflow Insight)

Workflow Insight is **opt-in**. Without it, customers still have:

- The existing `ListDurableExecutions` API for basic execution listing (status, start time, name)
- The existing `GetDurableExecutionHistory` API for individual execution inspection
- Standard Lambda CloudWatch Metrics (invocations, duration, errors)
- Standard Lambda CloudWatch Logs (runtime logs from their code)
- X-Ray traces (if enabled)

These existing capabilities cover single-execution debugging but do not support cross-execution queries, step-level analytics, custom business dimensions, or curated operational data at scale. Workflow Insight fills that gap for customers who need it.

### Minimum to opt in

Enabling Workflow Insight requires **one line of configuration** — add a single plugin with a destination:

```typescript
plugins: [new CloudWatchLogsInsight({ logGroupName: "/aws/durable/my-app" })];
```

That's it. With this single line:

- All completed executions emit a curated summary record to the specified CloudWatch log group
- All fields included by default (status, duration, steps, errors)
- No sampling (100% of executions)
- Finished-only mode (one record per execution, no in-progress updates)
- The Workflow Insight UI in the Console immediately works against this log group

No IAM changes needed if customers use the Lambda function's existing CloudWatch log group (the execution role already has write access to it). If they specify a different log group or a different destination (S3, DynamoDB, etc.), they'll need to grant the execution role appropriate permissions. No infrastructure to provision. No schema to define. Customers can then progressively customize: add sampling, exclude fields, add destinations, enable in-progress mode — but none of that is required to get started.

## Appendix: Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does enabling Workflow Insight add latency to my workflow execution?**
A: No. Emission is asynchronous and non-blocking. The execution engine's performance guarantees are unchanged. If a destination is slow or unavailable, the workflow continues unaffected — data may be buffered or dropped (configurable), but execution never waits.

**Q: What happens if my destination is down?**
A: Behavior is configurable per plugin. Options include: buffer and retry with backoff, drop and increment a CloudWatch metric counting dropped records, or best-effort fire-and-forget. The workflow execution is never impacted regardless of destination health.

**Q: Can I use this with existing executions, or only new ones?**
A: Initial launch supports new executions only — data is emitted for executions started after the feature is enabled. Backfill of historical executions is planned for a subsequent release.

**Q: Can I send data to multiple destinations at once?**
A: Yes. The plugin architecture supports multiple plugins simultaneously. For example, you can emit to S3 for cheap archival and CloudWatch Logs for real-time debugging, each with different field selections or sampling rates.

**Q: How does sampling work? Will I get incomplete records?**
A: Sampling is decided per execution at start time. If an execution is sampled in, all its data is emitted (every status change, every step). If sampled out, nothing is emitted. You never get partial or fragmented records for a single execution.

**Q: Can I attach my own business data (customer ID, order amount, region) to the emitted records?**
A: Yes. The SDK provides a `setAttributes()` API to attach arbitrary key-value metadata from your workflow code. These custom fields are included in the emitted record and are queryable in your destination.

**Q: Do I need to change my workflow code to enable this?**
A: Minimal change — you add a plugin to your SDK configuration (1-3 lines). Your actual workflow logic (steps, waits, callbacks) remains unchanged.

**Q: What if my execution input/output is too large for the destination?**
A: Each plugin uses a default size limit appropriate for its destination (e.g., 256KB for CloudWatch Logs, 400KB for DynamoDB). Data is truncated with a clear marker indicating truncation occurred. You can fine-tune the limit per field.

**Q: Can I use the Workflow Insight UI with Datadog/Splunk/Snowflake?**
A: The AWS Console and VS Code UI work out-of-the-box with AWS destinations only (S3, CloudWatch, DynamoDB, Aurora). For third-party destinations, use the open-source UI library and implement a query adapter for your platform.

**Q: How does this relate to X-Ray / CloudWatch Logs I already have for Lambda?**
A: Complementary, not competing. X-Ray traces individual invocations. CloudWatch Logs captures runtime output. Workflow Insight provides cross-execution operational and business intelligence — "how many failed this week", "what's the average duration by region", "which step is the bottleneck." Each emitted record includes correlation IDs to link back to X-Ray traces and CloudWatch log groups.

**Q: Can third-party vendors build plugins for their platform?**
A: Yes. The plugin interface is public and documented. Vendors (Datadog, Grafana, etc.) or community contributors can publish their own destination plugins with custom delivery guarantees, batching, and authentication.

**Q: What's the cost impact of enabling this?**
A: Depends on your configuration. Key cost drivers: number of executions × sampling rate × record size × destination pricing. Use sampling (e.g., 5%) and field selection to control cost. We will provide guidance on estimating cost for common configurations.

## Appendix: Plugin Maintenance Investment

**Q: How much investment is required to build and maintain these plugins?**

The investment is real but bounded by design:

**What we build and maintain:**

- Plugin interface contract — small surface area (`emit(record)`, `flush()`, `configure(options)`), stable, rarely changes
- 6 AWS destination plugins — S3, DynamoDB, CloudWatch Logs, Aurora, Kinesis, EventBridge. These target stable AWS APIs that rarely introduce breaking changes. Maintenance is low once built.
- 1 OTel log plugin — covers the entire long tail of third-party destinations (Datadog, Splunk, Grafana, New Relic, etc.) through a single implementation against the stable OTel spec
- Open-source UI component library — React components, versioned, community-contributed improvements
- Documentation and getting-started guides

**What we don't build or maintain:**

- Third-party destination plugins — vendors (Datadog, Splunk, etc.) or community members build and own these. We define the interface; they own the implementation.
- Customer-specific query adapters for the UI
- Customer-hosted UI instances or custom dashboards

**Why the maintenance burden is manageable:**

1. AWS service APIs are stable — S3 PUT, DynamoDB PutItem, CloudWatch PutLogEvents haven't changed in years
2. OTel spec is stable — one plugin covers dozens of destinations without per-vendor maintenance
3. Plugin interface is intentionally minimal — small contract = fewer breaking changes
4. Open-source model shares maintenance — community contributes bug fixes, improvements, and new plugins. We review and merge, but we're not the sole authors.
5. We explicitly chose NOT to build a query API or internal data store — those would require ongoing scaling, security patching, and operational support that far exceeds plugin maintenance

**Estimated ongoing effort:** After initial build, maintenance is primarily dependency updates, occasional bug fixes from community reports, and documentation updates for new SDK versions. This is comparable to maintaining any SDK extension — not a new service.

### Key pricing observations

- **S3 is cheapest for writes** — PUT cost is per-request ($0.005/1000), independent of payload size. Storage cost is separate ($0.023/GB/month).
- **DynamoDB scales with payload size** — write cost is per-WCU (1 WCU = 1KB), so a 100KB record costs 100x more to write than a 1KB record.
- **CloudWatch Logs scales with data volume** — ingestion is $0.50/GB, so cost is proportional to total bytes ingested.
- **Kinesis is per-record** — $0.044 per million PUT records (on-demand), plus $0.08/GB payload. Cheapest for high-volume small records.
- **Datadog is moderate** — $0.10/GB ingestion plus ~$1.70/million events for indexing. Competitive with CloudWatch Logs on ingestion, but indexing adds up.
- **Splunk Cloud is the most expensive** — ~$0.80/GB/day ingestion. At high volumes with large payloads, costs escalate quickly.
- **Grafana Cloud is competitive with CloudWatch Logs** — ~$0.50/GB, similar volume-based pricing.
- **Sampling reduces cost linearly** — 10% sampling on 1M executions costs the same as 100K executions at 100%.

### Lambda compute cost for plugin execution

The Insight plugin runs within the same Lambda invocation as the workflow. While it does not add latency to the workflow's critical path (emission is asynchronous and non-blocking to the workflow logic), the compute time spent on light data preparation and sending records to the destination is billed as part of the Lambda invocation duration. This cost is minimal — typically a few milliseconds per emission — but at high volume it is non-zero and applies to the customer's Lambda bill.

To minimize both cost and destination writes, the plugin uses an internal queue with deduplication. In in-progress mode, each status change produces a new version of the full curated record (complete state, not a delta). Since newer records supersede older ones, intermediate records in the queue are dropped — only the latest state is sent when the queue flushes. This means 100 rapid status changes do NOT result in 100 destination writes; they collapse into a single write of the latest state. The queue flushes in batches at configurable intervals or when the execution suspends/completes. Customers should still factor in Lambda compute cost, especially for in-progress mode, but the batching significantly reduces both destination cost and network overhead.

_Note: Storage retention costs and query costs (Athena scans, DynamoDB reads, Log Insights queries) are not included above and will be covered in a separate analysis._

### Key querying observations

- **DynamoDB is cheapest for single-execution lookups** — point reads by execution ID cost fractions of a cent. But full-table scans are extremely expensive and impractical at scale.
- **S3 + Athena is cheapest for cross-execution analytics** — $5/TB scanned. Using Parquet format + date partitioning can reduce scanned data by 10-100x (e.g., scanning 1 day of 365 = 99.7% reduction).
- **Aurora Serverless v2 is the best all-rounder** — supports both fast point lookups (indexed) and complex cross-execution queries (SQL with WHERE, GROUP BY, JOIN) at moderate cost. Scales to zero when idle. Charges per ACU-hour (~$0.12/ACU-hour) — query cost depends on complexity and data touched.
- **CloudWatch Log Insights is simple but scales linearly** — $0.005/GB scanned per query. No optimization levers beyond time-range filtering.
- **Third-party platforms (Datadog, Splunk, Grafana Cloud) include query cost in their ingestion/subscription pricing** — no per-query charge, but you've already paid at ingestion time.
- **DynamoDB is not designed for cross-execution queries** — it excels at "get execution by ID" but cannot efficiently answer "show me all failed executions this week" without a full scan or GSI.
